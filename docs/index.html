Overview

This project extends off the prior landscape analysis conducted by Potochnick et al. (2024) through the University of North Carolina at Charlotte’s Social Aspects of Health Initiative (SAHI), an interdisciplinary, community-centered research partnership between university faculty, students, and key Charlotte-area community stakeholders.

The original study’s strived to understand the geographic, socioeconomic and cultural barriers that contributed to the under-utilization of subsidized Prenatal to Early Childhood Education (ECE) services among eligible Black and Hispanic families in the Charlotte-Mecklenburg area of North Carolina Potochnick et al. (2024).

The study responded to calls from community leaders for targeted research into child development disparities affecting low-income Black and Latino families in Charlotte-Mecklenburg. ECE ulitization is significant as it is a tool meant to promote long-term economic mobility and Charlotte was placed last for intergenerational economic mobility out of the 50 largest U.S. cities Potochnick et al. (2024).

The study was separated into three separate projects, the second being a service landscape assessment meant to create an inventory of services for children age 0 to 3 years old and analyze accessibility with additional insights provided by low-income providers and families. The study concluded that that accessibility did not differ significantly spatially or across socioeconomic statuses Potochnick et al. (2024).

This project aims to provide a reproducible documentation that quantify these findings through a targeted spatial analysis, shifting the focus from where facilities and populations are located to the real-world barriers and decisions that shape whether families actually use them. Specifically, the analysis measures transit and car-based accessibility to ECE daycare facilities for children under 6, weighed against facility capacity and filtered by quality ratings and subsidy acceptance.

Methods

To perform this targeted analysis, this project uses:

  • A Two-Step Floating Catchment Area (2SFCA) model via the accessibility package, which generates an accessibility index by accounting for provider-to-population ratios across space

  • A travel-time matrix via the r5r package, which simulates realistic transit and driving behavior across street networks

  • A Principal Component Analysis (PCA) using FactoMineR that combines Census data from tidycensus to capture correlations between accessibility scores and vulnerability indicators

Key Findings

  • Accessibility declines substantially when restricting supply to subsidized high-quality providers.
  • Transit-based access to childcare is limited across much of Mecklenburg County.
  • PCA results suggest childcare accessibility operates partly independently from broader socioeconomic vulnerability patterns.
